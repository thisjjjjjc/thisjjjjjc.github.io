TimeCapsule

Overview

This project was completed during the summer of 2021 at Snap Research, where our team explored the design of effortful communication, specifically focusing on encouraging people to invest meaningful effort into crafting augmented reality (AR) experiences for each other. We conceptualized a memory box that invites a group of friends to decorate it with photos, thus fostering the feeling of memory keepsaking among friend groups. By integrating internal technical functionalities such as object auto-crop, gesture detection, and photo-taking into the experience, we productized our ideas into a Snapchat Lens, called TimeCapsule.

Concept

While digital communication tools are woven into our lives, we still often feel disconnected. Studies show that meaningful interaction can be fostered through effort, similar to activities like gifting, decorating photo books together, or crafting a collage board. In this project, we aim to translate these group crafting activities from the analog world into the digital experience. For keepsaking memories among friend groups, we think about a TimeCapsule.

Keepsake the artifacts:
Timecapsule is a magic container collects artifacts as keepsakes, It could be filled with a photo, a hand-written letter, or a hand-made gift.

Re-visiting the moments:
When you re-opened it and see those keepsakes you put in, you can feel like travel back to those moments.

What should be 'Timecapsule' look like?
We explored different formats for the representation‚Äîa tree, a jar, a plane, a box, or even the use of real-world planes, such as walls and windows. I created each 3D model and simulated it in LensStudio to see how it felt when decorating each one. We realized the tree and jar seemed to have limited creative space for people to add content due to their limited physical space and the common understanding of how we engage with them. Then, we decided to go 'less'‚Äîchoosing planes. We also considered how much freedom we wanted to give the users: we hope not to limit users to interact with just any planes, but also not to constrain them with a preset template. Then we added a constraint with a box, which has 12 planes but the creative space is limited within the box.

Prototype

How can we interact with the box?
As the time capsule, we first need to decorate it, and then close and store it. Considering the nature of the box, which has six external faces and internal space, I began prototyping how we can decorate it. I explored how technical building blocks such as object auto-crop, gesture detection, and photo-taking can be integrated into the box experience.

Add photos on the box
Adding photos from album would be the basic functionalities that allow people to decorate the box - they can both take a photo or draw from the albums.

Capture the objects
By using the auto-crop functionality, users can crop near-field objects by scanning them.

Open/close the box by hand gestures
Using buttons to trigger the open and close can be most robust and easy way, but I am thinking about how can we make use of AR? since the hand gesture recognitions is avaliable, then I tried to prototype the experience of openning and closing box with hand gestures.

Onboarding
Since we are including some novel interactions, such as gesture detection and auto-crop functionality, I created an onboarding experience that guides users to become familiar with (1) How they can interact with the box (decorating the outside of the box, keeping objects inside the box); (2) the new interactions (gesture detection and cropping the image) before they start decorating the box.

Novel
but still
Intuitive

In this project, the challenge during prototyping is designing the experience to make use of AR and push towards 'being novel' while still keeping the interaction intuitive. We were exploring some physical interactions with AR mobiles. Given the computer vision technical capabilities from the engineering side, we are able to perform hand tracking and hand gesture detection. We can also do physical mobile detection, such as tilt left and right detection. I was prototyping these novel alternatives to substitute the default button-based mobile interaction.

Add gesture detection for simple triggers to add playfulness.
When prototyping the finger tracking functionality and testing out the idea of 'using the pinky finger to magnetically collect each board and move the box board,' we realized the user experience could be challenging. Due to the fact that our left hand is holding the phone, it makes moving with the right hand a bit difficult and seems to burden the users. However, if physical interaction serves only as a simple trigger, it feels fun and still intuitive.

Editting functionalities are better to be intuitive and default
Editing functionalities (e.g., adding a photo, cropping a photo, picking a photo) are better when they are intuitive and set as defaults. Testing out some novel ideas, I tried using finger gestures (such as scissors for cropping or an 'X' for deleting) to trigger the editing functionalities in the experience. However, during the internal pilot, people feel the user experience was not pleasant due to inaccurate detection or time delays in detection, which increase the difficulty people face to decorate the box. After trials, we decided to use more default options‚Äîscreen-based and button interactions.
